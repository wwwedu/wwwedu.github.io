Seemingly Unrelated Regression (SUR)

Proofs and Derivations

Author

Dragos Ailoae

Published

January 25, 2026

1 Introduction to SUR Systems

In the analysis of Seemingly Unrelated Regression (SUR) systems, we examine multiple regression equations that appear independent but may have correlated error terms. The key question is: when does Generalized Least Squares (GLS) provide efficiency gains over Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)?

This document proves that in two important special cases, GLS estimation offers no efficiency gains and yields identical estimates to equation-by-equation OLS.

2 The Three SUR Cases

In SUR analysis, three distinct cases arise based on the structure of error correlations and regressors:

2.1 Case 1: Contemporaneously Uncorrelated Disturbances

The disturbances are uncorrelated across equations: \[ E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \mathbf{0}, \quad \text{for } i \neq j \]

2.2 Case 2: Correlated Disturbances with Identical Regressors

The disturbances are contemporaneously correlated and all equations share the same regressors: \[ E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \sigma_{ij} \mathbf{I}_T \neq \mathbf{0} \] where \(\mathbf{I}_T\) is a \(T \times T\) identity matrix, and \[ \mathbf{X}_i = \mathbf{X}_j, \quad \text{for all } i, j \]

2.3 Case 3: Correlated Disturbances with Different Regressors

The disturbances are contemporaneously correlated with different regressors: \[ E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \sigma_{ij} \mathbf{I}_T \neq \mathbf{0} \] and \[ \mathbf{X}_i \neq \mathbf{X}_j, \quad \text{for at least some } i \text{ and } j \]

Main Result

In Cases 1 and 2, there are no efficiency gains from GLS, and the estimator is identical to OLS.

Only in Case 3 does SUR estimation provide efficiency improvements over OLS.

3 Proof for Case 1: No Contemporaneous Correlation

3.1 Setup

When \(\sigma_{12} = \sigma_{21} = 0\), the variance-covariance matrix is block diagonal: \[ \boldsymbol{\Omega} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_T = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{22} \end{bmatrix} \otimes \mathbf{I}_T \]

3.2 Derivation Using Block Diagonal Structure

The GLS estimator has the general form: \[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \left(\mathbf{X}^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{y} \]

For the two-equation case: \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_{1} \\ \mathbf{b}_{2} \end{bmatrix} &= \left[\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2 \end{pmatrix}\right]^{-1} \\ &\quad \times \left[\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{y}_1 \\ \mathbf{y}_2 \end{pmatrix}\right] \\[1em] &= \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2 \end{pmatrix}^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_1 \\ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_2 \end{pmatrix} \\[1em] &= \begin{pmatrix} \left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_1 \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_2 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned} \]

Since \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{ii} = \sigma_{ii} \mathbf{I}_T\), it factors out: \[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}} \]

3.3 Alternative Derivation Using Greene’s Expanded Form

Greene’s expanded SUR estimator uses elements \(\sigma^{ij}\) from \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\): \[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2} \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \]

When \(\sigma^{12} = \sigma^{21} = 0\): \[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2} \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \]

3.4 Interpretation

Case 1 Takeaway

If the models are truly unrelated (uncorrelated disturbances), then:

  • The GLS/SUR estimator reduces to equation-by-equation OLS
  • There is no efficiency gain from joint estimation
  • Inference procedures are identical whether estimated jointly or separately

4 Proof for Case 2: Identical Regressors

4.1 Two-Equation Case

Assume \(\mathbf{X}_{1} = \mathbf{X}_{2}\) but disturbances are correlated. Starting from Greene’s expanded form:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) & \sigma^{12} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) \\ \sigma^{21} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) & \sigma^{22} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \\[1em] &= \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)\right]^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} \]

Using the Kronecker product property \((\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{B}^{-1}\):

\[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1}\right] \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \]

4.1.1 Substitution Using OLS Estimates

From equation-by-equation OLS: \[ \mathbf{b}_{j} = \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j}, \quad j = 1, 2 \]

This implies: \[ \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) \mathbf{b}_{j} = \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j} \]

Substituting \(\mathbf{X}_{j}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j}\) in the GLS formula:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1}\right] \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \\[1em] &= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} & \sigma_{12} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \\ \sigma_{21} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} & \sigma_{22} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \\ \sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} \]

Expanding the matrix multiplication:

\[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_{1}\left(\sigma_{11} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{21}\right) + \mathbf{b}_{2}\left(\sigma_{11} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{22}\right) \\ \mathbf{b}_{1}\left(\sigma_{21} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{21}\right) + \mathbf{b}_{2}\left(\sigma_{21} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{22}\right) \end{bmatrix} \]

4.1.2 Key Simplification

The terms in parentheses simplify using \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = \mathbf{I}\):

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \sigma_{12} \\ \sigma_{21} & \sigma_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{11} & \sigma^{12} \\ \sigma^{21} & \sigma^{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

This yields: \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{11} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{21} &= 1 \\ \sigma_{11} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{22} &= 0 \\ \sigma_{21} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{21} &= 0 \\ \sigma_{21} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{22} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

Therefore: \[ \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_{1} \\ \mathbf{b}_{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}} \]

4.2 General Case: \(M\) Equations with Identical Regressors

For \(M\) equations where \(\mathbf{X}_{j} \equiv \mathbf{X}_{0}\) (an \(N \times K^{*}\) matrix with \(K^{*} = K/M\)), the stacked regressor matrix is:

\[ \underset{(MN \times K)}{\mathbf{X}} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_{0} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{X}_{0} \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0} \]

The GLS estimator becomes:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left(\mathbf{X}^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{y} \\[0.5em] &= \left(\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right) \mathbf{y} \end{aligned} \]

Using the Kronecker product property \((\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})(\mathbf{C} \otimes \mathbf{D}) = \mathbf{AC} \otimes \mathbf{BD}\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime}\right) \mathbf{y} \\[0.5em] &= \left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime}\right) \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{y}_{M} \end{pmatrix} \\[0.5em] &= \begin{pmatrix} \left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{M} \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}} \end{aligned} \]

4.3 Interpretation

Case 2 Takeaway

Even when disturbances are correlated across equations, if all equations use the same regressors:

  • GLS estimates equal OLS estimates
  • Joint estimation provides no coefficient efficiency gain
  • This result may seem counterintuitive but follows from the algebraic structure
  • However, joint estimation still enables valid cross-equation hypothesis tests

5 Summary and Practical Implications

5.1 Main Results

Case Error Correlation Regressors \(\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}}\)? Efficiency Gain?
1 None Any Yes No
2 Present Identical Yes No
3 Present Different No Yes

5.2 When SUR Improves Efficiency

SUR and Efficiency

SUR provides efficiency gains only when:

  1. Disturbances are contemporaneously correlated across equations, and
  2. Equations contain different regressors

A helpful rule of thumb: correlated errors are not enough.

5.3 Practical Example: Demand System

Consider a system of household demand equations: \[ \begin{aligned} \text{housing} &= \beta_{10} + \beta_{11}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{12}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{13}\text{clothprc} + \beta_{14}\text{income} + \beta_{15}\text{size} + \beta_{16}\text{age} + u_1 \\ \text{food} &= \beta_{20} + \beta_{21}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{22}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{23}\text{clothprc} + \beta_{24}\text{income} + \beta_{25}\text{size} + \beta_{26}\text{age} + u_2 \\ \text{clothing} &= \beta_{30} + \beta_{31}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{32}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{33}\text{clothprc} + \beta_{34}\text{income} + \beta_{35}\text{size} + \beta_{36}\text{age} + u_3 \end{aligned} \]

All three equations use identical regressors but different dependent variables.

5.3.1 What SUR Does Not Do Here

Because the regressor matrices are identical:

  • SUR does not improve coefficient efficiency
  • GLS estimates equal equation-by-equation OLS
  • This is an application of Case 2

In short, SUR cannot extract additional information from correlated errors alone.

5.3.2 Why Demand Systems Are Still Estimated Jointly

Demand systems are estimated jointly not for efficiency, but for economic structure.

Joint estimation allows researchers to:

  • Impose cross-equation restrictions implied by theory:
    • Adding-up
    • Homogeneity
    • Symmetry
  • Estimate the cross-equation error covariance matrix
  • Conduct valid system-wide hypothesis tests

Once such restrictions are imposed, we are estimating a different (constrained) model, so the resulting estimates generally differ from unrestricted OLS or unrestricted SUR.

Efficiency vs. Structure
  • SUR for efficiency requires different regressors
  • SUR for economic theory arises when coefficients are linked across equations

Demand systems rely primarily on the second, not the first.

6 Appendix: Kronecker Product Properties

For conformable matrices \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), \(\mathbf{C}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\): \[ (\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{B}^{-1} \] \[ (\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})(\mathbf{C} \otimes \mathbf{D}) = \mathbf{AC} \otimes \mathbf{BD} \]

See Greene’s Appendix A for comprehensive treatment.

# Introduction to SUR Systems

In the analysis of Seemingly Unrelated Regression (SUR) systems, we examine multiple regression equations that appear independent but may have correlated error terms. The key question is: **when does Generalized Least Squares (GLS) provide efficiency gains over Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)?**

This document proves that in two important special cases, GLS estimation offers **no efficiency gains** and yields **identical estimates** to equation-by-equation OLS.

# The Three SUR Cases

In SUR analysis, three distinct cases arise based on the structure of error correlations and regressors:

## Case 1: Contemporaneously Uncorrelated Disturbances

The disturbances are uncorrelated across equations: $$
E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \mathbf{0}, \quad \text{for } i \neq j
$$

## Case 2: Correlated Disturbances with Identical Regressors

The disturbances are contemporaneously correlated **and** all equations share the same regressors: $$
E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \sigma_{ij} \mathbf{I}_T \neq \mathbf{0}
$$ where $\mathbf{I}_T$ is a $T \times T$ identity matrix, and $$
\mathbf{X}_i = \mathbf{X}_j, \quad \text{for all } i, j
$$

## Case 3: Correlated Disturbances with Different Regressors

The disturbances are contemporaneously correlated with different regressors: $$
E\left(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j^{\prime}\right) = \sigma_{ij} \mathbf{I}_T \neq \mathbf{0}
$$ and $$
\mathbf{X}_i \neq \mathbf{X}_j, \quad \text{for at least some } i \text{ and } j
$$

::: callout-important
## Main Result

**In Cases 1 and 2, there are no efficiency gains from GLS, and the estimator is identical to OLS.**

Only in Case 3 does SUR estimation provide efficiency improvements over OLS.
:::

# Proof for Case 1: No Contemporaneous Correlation {#sec-case1}

## Setup

When $\sigma_{12} = \sigma_{21} = 0$, the variance-covariance matrix is block diagonal: $$
\boldsymbol{\Omega} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_T =
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_{11} & 0 \\
0 & \sigma_{22}
\end{bmatrix} \otimes \mathbf{I}_T
$$

## Derivation Using Block Diagonal Structure

The GLS estimator has the general form: $$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \left(\mathbf{X}^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{-1} \mathbf{y}
$$

For the two-equation case: $$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{b}_{1} \\
\mathbf{b}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} 
&= \left[\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2
\end{pmatrix}\right]^{-1} \\
&\quad \times \left[\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{y}_1 \\
\mathbf{y}_2
\end{pmatrix}\right] \\[1em]
&= \begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2
\end{pmatrix}^{-1}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_1 \\
\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_2
\end{pmatrix} \\[1em]
&= \begin{pmatrix}
\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_1 \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \boldsymbol{\Omega}_{22}^{-1} \mathbf{y}_2
\end{pmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{ii} = \sigma_{ii} \mathbf{I}_T$, it factors out: $$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}}
$$

## Alternative Derivation Using Greene's Expanded Form

Greene's expanded SUR estimator uses elements $\sigma^{ij}$ from $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}$: $$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

When $\sigma^{12} = \sigma^{21} = 0$: $$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
\left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Interpretation

::: callout-note
## Case 1 Takeaway

If the models are truly unrelated (uncorrelated disturbances), then:

-   The GLS/SUR estimator reduces to equation-by-equation OLS
-   There is **no efficiency gain** from joint estimation
-   Inference procedures are identical whether estimated jointly or separately
:::

# Proof for Case 2: Identical Regressors {#sec-case2}

## Two-Equation Case

Assume $\mathbf{X}_{1} = \mathbf{X}_{2}$ but disturbances are correlated. Starting from Greene's expanded form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) & \sigma^{12} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) \\
\sigma^{21} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) & \sigma^{22} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)
\end{bmatrix}^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} \\[1em]
&= \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)\right]^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

Using the Kronecker product property $(\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{B}^{-1}$:

$$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1}\right]
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### Substitution Using OLS Estimates

From equation-by-equation OLS: $$
\mathbf{b}_{j} = \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j}, \quad j = 1, 2
$$

This implies: $$
\left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right) \mathbf{b}_{j} = \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j}
$$

Substituting $\mathbf{X}_{j}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{j}$ in the GLS formula:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left[\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1}\right]
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} \\[1em]
&= \begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_{11} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} & \sigma_{12} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \\
\sigma_{21} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} & \sigma_{22} \left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{12} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2} \\
\sigma^{21} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{1} + \sigma^{22} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1} \mathbf{b}_{2}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

Expanding the matrix multiplication:

$$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{b}_{1}\left(\sigma_{11} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{21}\right) + \mathbf{b}_{2}\left(\sigma_{11} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{22}\right) \\
\mathbf{b}_{1}\left(\sigma_{21} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{21}\right) + \mathbf{b}_{2}\left(\sigma_{21} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{22}\right)
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### Key Simplification

The terms in parentheses simplify using $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = \mathbf{I}$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_{11} & \sigma_{12} \\
\sigma_{21} & \sigma_{22}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma^{11} & \sigma^{12} \\
\sigma^{21} & \sigma^{22}
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This yields: $$
\begin{aligned}
\sigma_{11} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{21} &= 1 \\
\sigma_{11} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{12} \sigma^{22} &= 0 \\
\sigma_{21} \sigma^{11} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{21} &= 0 \\
\sigma_{21} \sigma^{12} + \sigma_{22} \sigma^{22} &= 1
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore: $$
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{b}_{1} \\
\mathbf{b}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
\left(\mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{1}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{1}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{2}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{2}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2}
\end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}}
$$

## General Case: $M$ Equations with Identical Regressors

For $M$ equations where $\mathbf{X}_{j} \equiv \mathbf{X}_{0}$ (an $N \times K^{*}$ matrix with $K^{*} = K/M$), the stacked regressor matrix is:

$$
\underset{(MN \times K)}{\mathbf{X}} = \begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{X}_{0} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{X}_{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{X}_{0}
\end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}
$$

The GLS estimator becomes:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left(\mathbf{X}^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{y} \\[0.5em]
&= \left(\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right)\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{\prime}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{I}_{N}\right) \mathbf{y}
\end{aligned}
$$

Using the Kronecker product property $(\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})(\mathbf{C} \otimes \mathbf{D}) = \mathbf{AC} \otimes \mathbf{BD}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} &= \left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime}\right) \mathbf{y} \\[0.5em]
&= \left(\mathbf{I}_{M} \otimes \left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime}\right)
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{y}_{M}
\end{pmatrix} \\[0.5em]
&= \begin{pmatrix}
\left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{1} \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{2} \\
\vdots \\
\left(\mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_{0}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_{0}^{\prime} \mathbf{y}_{M}
\end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}}
\end{aligned}
$$

## Interpretation

::: callout-note
## Case 2 Takeaway

Even when disturbances are correlated across equations, if all equations use the same regressors:

-   GLS estimates equal OLS estimates
-   Joint estimation provides **no coefficient efficiency gain**
-   This result may seem counterintuitive but follows from the algebraic structure
-   However, joint estimation still enables valid **cross-equation hypothesis tests**
:::

# Summary and Practical Implications

## Main Results

| Case | Error Correlation | Regressors | $\mathbf{b}_{\text{GLS}} = \mathbf{b}_{\text{OLS}}$? | Efficiency Gain? |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | None | Any | Yes | No |
| 2 | Present | Identical | Yes | No |
| 3 | Present | Different | No | **Yes** |

## When SUR Improves Efficiency

::: callout-tip
## SUR and Efficiency

SUR provides efficiency gains **only when**:

1.  Disturbances are contemporaneously correlated across equations, **and**
2.  Equations contain different regressors

A helpful rule of thumb: **correlated errors are not enough**.
:::

## Practical Example: Demand System

Consider a system of household demand equations: $$
\begin{aligned}
\text{housing} &= \beta_{10} + \beta_{11}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{12}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{13}\text{clothprc}
+ \beta_{14}\text{income} + \beta_{15}\text{size} + \beta_{16}\text{age} + u_1 \\
\text{food} &= \beta_{20} + \beta_{21}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{22}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{23}\text{clothprc}
+ \beta_{24}\text{income} + \beta_{25}\text{size} + \beta_{26}\text{age} + u_2 \\
\text{clothing} &= \beta_{30} + \beta_{31}\text{houseprc} + \beta_{32}\text{foodprc} + \beta_{33}\text{clothprc}
+ \beta_{34}\text{income} + \beta_{35}\text{size} + \beta_{36}\text{age} + u_3
\end{aligned}
$$

All three equations use **identical regressors** but different dependent variables.

### What SUR Does *Not* Do Here

Because the regressor matrices are identical:

-   SUR does **not** improve coefficient efficiency
-   GLS estimates equal equation-by-equation OLS
-   This is an application of **Case 2**

In short, SUR cannot extract additional information from correlated errors alone.

### Why Demand Systems Are Still Estimated Jointly

Demand systems are estimated jointly **not for efficiency**, but for **economic structure**.

Joint estimation allows researchers to:

-   Impose **cross-equation restrictions** implied by theory:
    -   Adding-up
    -   Homogeneity
    -   Symmetry
-   Estimate the **cross-equation error covariance matrix**
-   Conduct valid **system-wide hypothesis tests**

Once such restrictions are imposed, we are estimating a **different (constrained) model**, so the resulting estimates generally differ from unrestricted OLS or unrestricted SUR.

::: callout-important
## Efficiency vs. Structure

-   **SUR for efficiency** requires different regressors
-   **SUR for economic theory** arises when coefficients are linked across equations

Demand systems rely primarily on the **second**, not the first.
:::

# Appendix: Kronecker Product Properties

For conformable matrices $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$, and $\mathbf{D}$: $$
(\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{B}^{-1}
$$ $$
(\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B})(\mathbf{C} \otimes \mathbf{D}) = \mathbf{AC} \otimes \mathbf{BD}
$$

See Greene's [Appendix A](http://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~wgreene/Text/Greene-EA-7&8ed-Appendices.pdf#page=24) for comprehensive treatment.
