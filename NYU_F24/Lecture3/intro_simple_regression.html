Intro to Simple Regression
¶

General Concepts¶

Much of econometric analysis begins with the following premise: let $Y$ and $X$ be two variables. We are interested in explaining $Y$ in terms of $X$. On the other hand, any single $X$ will never fully explain an economic variable $Y$. As such, all other non-observable factors, influencing $Y$, manifest themselves as an unpredictable (i.e. random) disturbance $\epsilon$. Thus, we are looking for a function $f$, which describes this relationship: $$ Y=f(X, \epsilon) $$ where:

  • $Y$ - is the variable of interest (e.g. total production, revenue, etc.);
  • $X$ - is a variable, which would help us explain $Y$ (examples of $X$ include labor input, number of hours worked, capital input, equipment quality, number of employees, raw materials etc.);

Furthermore:

  • $f(\cdot)$ is called a regression function;
  • $X$ is called the independent variable, the control variable, the explanatory variable, the predictor variable, or the regressor;
  • $Y$ is called the dependent variable, the response variable, the explained variable, the predicted variable or the regressand;
  • $\epsilon$ is called the random component, the error term, the disturbance or the (economic) shock. We will assume that $X$ is a random variable (though this may not always be the case). In any case, $Y$ is a random variable $(r . v .)$, which depends on $\epsilon(r . v .)$ and $X$. The simplest functional form of $f$ is linear:
\begin{equation} Y=\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X+\epsilon \end{equation}

If this functional form is appropriate for the data, our goal is to estimate its unknown coefficients $\beta_{0}$ and $\beta_{1}$ from the available data sample. Usually, we will assume that we have a finite random sample $\left(X_{1}, Y_{1}\right), \ldots,\left(X_{N}, Y_{N}\right)$ of independent identically distributed (i.i.d.) r.v.'s (unless stated otherwise), with their realizations (observed values) $\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right), \ldots,\left(x_{N}, y_{N}\right) .$ Since $\epsilon$ is a collection of unknown r.v.'s, unlike $X$ and $Y$, we do not observe $\epsilon$.

We expect that, on average, the relationship in (1) holds. The two-dimensional r.v. $(X, Y)$ is fully determined by the two-dimensional r.v. $(X, \epsilon) .$ In order to get good estimates of $\beta_{0}$ and $\beta_{1}$, we have to impose certain restrictions on the properties of $\epsilon$ and the interaction between $X$ and $\epsilon$.

We denote $\mathbb{E}(Y \mid X)$ - the conditional expectation of $Y$ on $X$ - an expectation of $Y$, provided we know the value of r.v. $X$

Equation (1) implies that if $\mathbb{E}(\epsilon \mid X)=0$, then the r.v. $Y=\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X+\epsilon$ is on average $\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X$ :

$$ \mathbb{E}\left(\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X+\epsilon \mid X\right)=\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X $$

and

  • $\beta_{0}$ - the intercept parameter, sometimes called the constant term - is the average value of $Y$ when $X=0$. It often has no econometric meaning, especially if $X$ cannot take a zero value (e.g. if $X$ is wage );
  • $\beta_{1}$ - the slope parameter - is the average change of $Y$ provided $X$ increases by 1 .

Regression and the Sample data¶

Assume that the relationship between $Y$ and $X$ (i.e. the data generating process) is a linear one as discussed before: $$ Y=\beta_{0}+\beta_{1} X+\epsilon $$ where $X$ and $\epsilon$ are r.v.'s. For simplicity assume that:

  • $\beta_{0}=10, \beta_{1}=2$
  • $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), X \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0,5^{2}\right)$
  • $\mathbb{E}(\epsilon \mid X)=0$ Observations (i.e. realizations) of the random sample $\left(X_{1}, Y_{1}\right), \ldots,\left(X_{N}, Y_{N}\right)$ with $N=50$ can be generated as follows:

Parameter Terminology¶

In general, we do not know the underlying true values of the coefficients $\beta_{0}$ and $\beta_{1}$. We will denote $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ - the estimation of the unknown parameter $\beta_{1}$. We note that we can talk about $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ in two ways:

  • $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ as a concrete estimated value, or more generally, as a result. Then we say that $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ is an estimate of $\beta_{1}$ based on an observed data sample;
  • $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ as a random variable, or more generally, as a rule for calculating an estimate based on observed data. Then we say that $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ is an estimator of $\beta_{1}$. When we are talking about $\widehat{\beta}_{1}$ as an estimator, we can also talk about its mean, $\mathbb{E}\left(\widehat{\beta}_{1}\right)$, variance $\operatorname{Var}\left(\widehat{\beta}_{1}\right)$ and distribution, which are very important when determining if a particular estimation method is better than an alternative one.

Alternate notation: You may also see the notation $\alpha \equiv \beta_0$ and $\beta_1 \equiv \beta$ with the corresponding estimates as $a \equiv \hat\beta_0$ and $b \equiv \hat \beta_1$. (Greene textbook for instance)

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)¶

Crash course in optimization:¶

Find the critical point(s) of function f defined by: $$ f(x, y)=x^{2}+y^{2} $$ We first find the first order partial derivatives: $$ f_{x}\equiv\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=2 x, \quad f_{y}\equiv\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=2 y $$ We now solve the following equations $f_{x}(x, y)=0$ and $f_{y}(x, y)=0$ simultaneously. $$ \begin{aligned} &f_{x}(x, y)=2 x=0 \\ &f_{y}(x, y)=2 y=0 \end{aligned} $$ The solution to the above system of equations is the ordered pair $(0,0)$.

Below is the graph of $f(x, y)=x^{2}+y^{2}$ and it looks that at the critical point $(0,0) f$ has a minimum value.

OLS Estimator Derivation¶

The mathematical problem is straightforward:

  • Given a set of n points $\left(\mathrm{X}_{\mathrm{i}}, \mathrm{Y}_{\mathrm{i}}\right)$ on a scatterplot

  • find the best-fit line, $\hat{Y}_{i}=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1X_{i}$

  • Such that the sum of squared errors in $\mathrm{Y}, \sum\left(Y_{i}-\hat{Y}_{i}\right)^{2}$ is minimized

The derivation proceeds as follows: for convenience, name the sum of squares "Q", \begin{equation} \min _{\hat\beta_0, \hat\beta_1} Q=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(Y_{i}-\hat{Y}\right)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(Y_{i}-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 X_{i}\right)^{2} \end{equation}

Then, $\mathrm{Q}$ will be minimized at the values of $\mathrm{\hat\beta_0}$ and $\mathrm{\hat\beta_1}$ for which $\partial Q / \partial \hat\beta_0=0$ and $\partial Q / \partial \hat\beta_1=0$. The first of these conditions is, \begin{equation} \frac{\partial Q}{\partial \hat\beta_0}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}-2\left(Y_{i}-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 X_{i}\right)=2\left(n \hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_{i}-\sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_{i}\right)=0 \end{equation}

which, if we divide through by 2 and solve for $\hat\beta_0$, becomes simply, \begin{equation} \hat\beta_0=\bar{Y}-\hat\beta_1 \bar{X} \end{equation} which says that the constant $\hat\beta_0$ (the $\mathrm{y}$-intercept) is set such that the line must go through the mean of $\mathrm{x}$ and $\mathrm{y}$. This makes sense, because this point is the "center" of the data cloud.

The second condition for minimizing $\mathrm{Q}$ is, \begin{equation} \frac{\partial Q}{\partial \hat\beta_1}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}-2 X_{i}\left(Y_{i}-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 X_{i}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}-2\left(X_{i} Y_{i}-\hat\beta_0 X_{i}-\hat\beta_1 X_{i}^{2}\right)=0 \end{equation} If we substitute the expression for $\hat\beta_0$ from (4) into (5), then we get,

\begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i} Y_{i}-X_{i} \bar{Y}+\hat\beta_1 X_{i} \bar{X}-\hat\beta_1 X_{i}^{2}\right)=0 \end{equation}

We can separate this into two sums, \begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i} Y_{i}-X_{i} \bar{Y}\right)-\hat\beta_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}^{2}-X_{i} \bar{X}\right)=0 \end{equation} which becomes directly, \begin{equation} \hat\beta_1=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i} Y_{i}-X_{i} \bar{Y}\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}^{2}-X_{i} \bar{X}\right)}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i} Y_{i}\right)-n \bar{X} \bar{Y}}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}^{2}\right)-n \bar{X}^{2}} \end{equation}

We can translate (8) into a more intuitively obvious form, by noting that \begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\bar{X}^{2}-X_{i} \bar{X}\right)=0 \quad \text { and } \quad \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\bar{X} \bar{Y}-Y_{i} \bar{X}\right)=0 \end{equation} so that $\hat\beta_1$ can be rewritten as the ratio of $\operatorname{Cov}(x, y)$ to $\operatorname{Var}(x)$ : \begin{equation} \hat\beta_1=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i} Y_{i}-X_{i} \bar{Y}\right)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\bar{X} \bar{Y}-Y_{i} \bar{X}\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}^{2}-X_{i} \bar{X}\right)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\bar{X}^{2}-X_{i} \bar{X}\right)} =\frac{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}-\bar{X}\right)\left(Y_{i}-\bar{Y}\right)}{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}-\bar{X}\right)^{2}} =\frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var(X)}}} \end{equation}

Intuition¶

Intercept¶

As we saw above, the implication of equation (4) is that $\hat\beta_0$ (the y -intercept) is set such that the line must go through the mean of x and y.

Another way to think about it is that the least-squares line is a seesaw anchored at the center of the cloud. Once we find slope $\hat\beta_1$ (which minimizes the sum of squared deviations), the y-intercept is incidental to whatever slope was determined to be optimal.

To illustrate this, let's first generate some data with our DGP (data generating process) as follows:

  • $\beta_{0}=10, \beta_{1}=0.5$
  • $X_{i}=i-1, \epsilon_{i} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0,1^{2}\right), i=1, \ldots, 51$

So although we moved the center of the data cloud, the slope estimate remained the same. Which means that, alternately, we can think of the OLS procedure as:

  1. "De-mean" our data to origin i.e. shift center to (0,0)
  2. Calculate the slope $\hat\beta_1$ of the OLS line that minimizes the squared deviations
  3. Shift everything back to the initial data cloud center
  4. What is the y-intercept now? $\hat\beta_0=\bar{Y}-\hat\beta_1 \bar{X}$

Takeaways:¶

  • $\beta_{0}$, the intercept parameter, is not very useful and often has no econometric meaning, especially if $X$ cannot take a zero value (e.g. if $X$ is wage ). In machine learning it is called "the bias" term in the sense that it is the link to the center our our data cloud: the data variance (the important signal) happens around this bias level.

  • However, in software, you should never run a regression without an intercept term: that option forces a 0 intercept and will result in a biased estimate of the slope coefficient.

  • The estimate for $\hat\beta_1$ is quite close to the actual parameter $\beta_{1}=0.5$ that we used in the data generating process. We will look at what "close" means in the statistical sense a bit later in the course.

Regression Coefficient vs Correlation Coefficient¶

Recall that the correlation coefficient is: $$ \rho_{X, Y}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_{X} \sigma_{Y}} $$

where $\sigma_{X}=\sqrt{Var(X)}$ and $\sigma_{Y}=\sqrt{Var(Y)}$

We've established that the slope coefficient for univariate regression is: $$ \hat\beta_1=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)} \equiv \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_{X} \sigma_{X}} $$

Which implies the following relationship between the regression slope coefficient and the correlation coefficient: $$ \hat\beta_1=\rho_{X, Y}\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X} $$
